# Preprint Review: Uploading a worm brain to a digital fly?

![Screenshot of a simulation: In the left side, a digital fly; in the
middle; a digital worm; in the left side, wave
signals](images/brainbody_01.jpg)

A [recent preprint on Biorxiv by Brunton, Abe, Hu and
Tuthill](https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.03.20.713233v1),
from the University of Washington, describes a curious experiment: a
digital fly, based on data from the *Drosophila*, was connected to the
digital replica of the "brain" (connectome) of a *C. elegans* worm. Then
the fly performed very realistic walking movement in its digital
world. Brain upload?!

Not so fast. The key here is how the brain and the body are
connected. The authors used a Neural Network to interface the two
systems, trained using Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL). According to the
authors, this shows the power of the DRL algorithm: it managed to generate
a meaningful output from a biologically implausible input. In a sense,
this paper reminds me of the classical paper about detecting
statistically significant signals from the brain of a dead fish
through pattern recognition algorithms - a cautionary tale about being
careful of what we ask of optimization algorithms.

The authors have [a thread on Bluesky describing the "digital sphinx"
preprint](https://bsky.app/profile/tuthill.bsky.social/post/3mhtqcrrq4s2b)
with more details. [The Transmitter
magazine](https://www.thetransmitter.org/systems-neuroscience/digital-sphinx-raises-questions-about-connectome-models/)
also has a summary of the findings.
